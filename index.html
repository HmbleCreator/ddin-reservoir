An earlier result reporting ARI = 0.9758 ("Semantic Pressure and Zero-Shot Generalization") was identified as an artifact of label leakage: the labeling function derived semantic axis labels from the same initial consonant used for acoustic encoding. The result measured consonant-class separation, not phonosemantic recovery. The paper has been retracted from Zenodo. Papers 1, 2, and 3 stand with correction notes where relevant.

Mechanistic Diagnosis

Why the mapping failed.

The reservoir demonstrably processes acoustic information. The negative result for semantic recovery does not mean dynamics fail to carry meaning. It means the semantic target was built on different foundations from the acoustic signal.

①

What acoustic formants encode: Place of articulation — where in the vocal tract a sound is made. F2 locus equations distinguish labial from velar from dental in a physically real coordinate system.

②

What the Monier-Williams taxonomy encodes: Surface semantic categories (Motion, Separation, Containment, Experiential, Transfer) assembled by 19th-century Western lexicographers from English glosses. Not built from articulatory structure.

③

The measured gap: Semantic-locus ARI = 0.0095. The two taxonomies are nearly orthogonal. Motion roots begin with velars, palatals, and dentals in roughly equal proportion. No acoustic feature can bridge orthogonal spaces.

④

What this means: The question was wrong, not the architecture. The right question is: what semantic space is native to the articulatory geometry — not what external categories it can predict. That is the open problem.

Open Problems

Where the program points next.

The 56 experiments defined a precise boundary. The reservoir carries articulatory signal. The Monier-Williams taxonomy cannot be reached from that signal. The open question is whether a semantics built from within the Sanskrit tradition — Pāṇini's kāraka system, the Dhātu semantic groupings, the Nirukta classifications — aligns with articulatory structure in a way that Monier-Williams does not.

This is an empirically testable hypothesis. It requires a pre-specified Sanskrit-tradition taxonomy, a falsification criterion stated before running any experiments, and the smallest possible test of one specific geometric prediction.

  • The geodesic question: Do roots from the same Pāṇinian semantic class trace geodesics with similar curvature properties in the articulatory space? If yes: meaning is in the shape of the path. Pre-register the falsification criterion before testing.
  • The Lagrangian of articulation: Formalize the transition cost function for the vocal tract. Pāṇini's Sandhi rules are empirical observations of this Lagrangian. The mathematical task is to derive it from vocal tract geometry and show the rules follow from it.
  • The BCM benchmark: Test BCM homeostasis stability on N-MNIST or DVS-CIFAR10. Connect the ESL engineering result to a standard neuromorphic benchmark where the community can verify and extend it.
  • The grounding-generalization question: Do physically grounded phonological representations generalize better to unseen phoneme combinations than statistically learned ones? This is the central testable claim of the theoretical framework.
